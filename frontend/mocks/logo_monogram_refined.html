Direction 05 · Refinement

The Monogram A,
done properly.

You picked the strongest direction for a reason — the italic serif A with an amber element intersecting the letterform is the only logo that works as a standalone symbol without a wordmark. Favicon. App icon. Podcast cover. Social avatar. Merchandise. Everywhere the full wordmark can't fit.

The first version was tentative — the amber sat beneath the A instead of committing to the intersection. Below are four ways the amber can actually go through the letterform. Same Playfair Black italic base. Same amber colour. Four different typographic gestures. Pick the one that feels right and we commit.

What this does
The amber bar runs through the exact middle of the A and extends 14% beyond the letterform on both sides. The A sits on top of the bar — the z-index puts the letter above the strike so the amber reads as passing behind the letterform, intersecting it visually at the crossbar region.
What it says
Signal cutting through. The amber is a horizon line, a data stream, a signal — and the A is the agency standing on top of it. Most dramatic of the four. Reads hardest from distance. Highest contrast.

My recommendation

Variation A —
The Strike.

Four good options. But only one does everything we need a logo to do.

The Strike passes through the A at the midpoint — the exact position your eye expects a crossbar — but extends beyond the letterform so the amber reads as a signal line the agency is intersecting with. It's the only variation where the relationship between the letter and the amber tells a story: the system (amber) runs horizontally, and the agency (A) is positioned on top of it, riding the signal.

The Crossbar (Variation B) is the most refined but the quietest. A typographic purist's choice — the amber is almost decoration rather than a deliberate element. Too subtle for a brand that needs to stand out from 80% of blue B2B logos.

The Foundation (Variation C) is the polite version. It's what I built the first time. Safer, warmer, grounded — but it doesn't feel like it's cutting through anything. The amber sits beneath the letter rather than meeting it.

The Full Bar (Variation D) is the most dramatic but hardest to use in tight layouts. The extended bar demands breathing room — it needs white space around it to work, and real-world layouts (nav bars, favicons, email signatures) rarely give it that room.

The Strike is the one that holds its power at every scale. At 260px it's striking. At 44px it's distinctive. At 13px it still reads as "A with an amber line through it." At favicon size the A alone is enough — the strike becomes part of the letterform's silhouette.

My call: Variation A — The Strike. If your gut says otherwise, tell me which one speaks to you and why. This is the kind of decision where your instinct matters more than my logic.
